Abstract

Much of the discourse surrounding blockchain technologies, such as cryptocurrencies, emphasizes enhanced privacy and censorship resistance through decentralization. In practice, however, the privacy and censorship boundaries experienced by users are shaped not only by the underlying cryptographic protocols, but also by the network-facing behavior of the client software through which blockchains are accessed.

In this paper, we present the first systematic study of DNS resolution behavior generated by cryptocurrency-supporting client software. We show that this behavior gives rise to application-specific DNS fingerprints that can be observed by network intermediaries. To characterize these fingerprints in practice, we conduct a large-scale measurement study of 40 popular cryptocurrency-supporting applications across 303 versions. We analyze these fingerprints and validate their real-world observability using passive DNS and DNS telemetry from URL filtering logs collected by a large network security provider.

Our results demonstrate that DNS activity generated during normal application operation exhibits stable, application-specific patterns that enable intermediaries to identify the application in use and infer sensitive user behavior. This undermines the privacy and censorship-resistance guarantees commonly attributed to blockchain-based systems.
